NBA: Losing Leading to Winning

Imagine you’re at a game. Your team is down six at halftime. Are you quietly confident your team will bounce back—or is it a sneak preview of how the game will end?

Question: How does “losing” at halftime affect win odds and performance vs expectations?
Data: 2024–25 NBA regular season (home-team perspective)
Sources: NBA public APIs, boxscore feeds, roster age from nbaage.com
Features: halftime margin, team strength, roster age, rest days

Claim

Expanding on Berger & Pope’s halftime findings, I argue that “losing” by itself only minimally shifts a team’s raw chance of winning, but it matters a lot more once you layer in context: team strength, roster age, and rest.

Background

  • Berger & Pope (“Can Losing Lead to Winning?”) suggest teams slightly behind at halftime may win more often due to motivation/effort.
  • Klein Teeselink et al. revisit the idea across sports with larger datasets and find the effect small/inconsistent and often not statistically reliable with tighter models.
  • Andrew Gelman argues the original effect may be overstated and highlights how modeling choices can make tiny effects look big.

Method

  • Perspective: home team. Halftime margin (home score − away score).
  • Outcome: whether the home team came back to win after trailing at halftime.
  • Strength metric: each team’s average home point differential (bucketed).
  • Roster age: merged team average roster age (bucketed: young / prime / old).
  • Rest: days since last game (bucketed: 0–1 / 2–3 / 4+).

Finding 1: Raw halftime win curve looks normal

My version of the halftime curve for the full 2024–25 season slopes upward smoothly: big halftime leads matter a lot. Near zero, being down a couple points nudges odds, but doesn’t flip the game by itself.

Finding 2: Strength changes what “down at half” means

Conditioning on games where the home team is losing at halftime, comeback rates depend heavily on team strength:

  • Strong: ~39% comeback rate
  • Middle: ~29%
  • Weak: ~13%

Finding 3: Older rosters are slightly better at comebacks

Again only for home teams trailing at halftime:

  • Old: ~33%
  • Prime: ~30%
  • Young: ~24%

Finding 4: Rest matters a lot

Among home teams trailing at halftime:

  • 0–1 days rest: ~18%
  • 2–3 days rest: ~31%
  • 4+ days rest: ~26% (possible rust or weird schedule spots)

Finding 5: Combining strength + age + rest shows the “rubber band” pattern

When combining all three factors (still only looking at teams losing at halftime), the difference is huge. Strong teams on normal rest come back roughly ~41–47% across age groups. Weak, young teams on normal rest flip the deficit only around ~6% of the time.

Conclusion

So, does losing lead to winning? Not in a universal way. On average, losing at halftime only modestly affects raw win probability and generally disadvantages you. But once you condition on context—who is trailing, how strong they are, how old they are, and how rested they are, the data is patterned.

Being down at half is close to a death sentence for weak, young, tired teams. For strong, rested teams, it’s closer to a challenge they’re likely to solve.
